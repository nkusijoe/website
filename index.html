Our Background

Mental health is described by WHO as: ... “a state of well-being in which the individual realizes his or her own abilities, can cope with the normal stresses of life, can work productively and fruitfully, and is able to make a contribution to his or her community” (WHO 2001a, p.1). In this positive sense mental health is the foundation for well-being and effective functioning for an individual and for a community. This core concept of mental health is consistent with its wide and varied interpretation across cultures. Since the genocide against the Tutsi in 1994, Rwanda has gone through years of sustained development and growth. However, the country continues to grapple with significant mental health challenges. A considerable proportion of the Rwandan population lives with trauma linked to the genocide against the Tutsi. Research in Rwanda has highlighted the magnitude of mental health problems. Around 29% of the general Rwandan population has been found to have post-traumatic stress disorders (Palmer and Firmin, 2011) and 15% has depression (Bolton et al, 2002). Mental illnesses are among the most common health conditions in Rwanda and around the world. Even the most serious mental health conditions can be treated, allowing people to better contribute to their families and communities. Safe place is an initiative founded by James Mugambe, seeking to create mental health awareness and to avail environment where individuals going through a stressful or traumatic experiences can be able to open up with a goal of healing.
